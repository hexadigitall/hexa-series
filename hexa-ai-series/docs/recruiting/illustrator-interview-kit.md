# Illustrator Interview Kit

**Purpose:** Structured interview script and evaluation prompts for illustrator candidates.

---

## Interview Goals

Use this conversation to verify five things:
1. The candidate can carry a recurring protagonist across multiple books.
2. The candidate understands educational storytelling, not just isolated illustration.
3. The candidate can work inside a structured production workflow.
4. The candidate can communicate clearly, take feedback, and hit deadlines.
5. The candidate is a real fit for the tone and ambition of HexaVerse.

---

## Interview Format

**Recommended duration:** 45-60 minutes

### Suggested structure
- 5 min: project overview
- 10 min: portfolio walkthrough
- 15 min: process and workflow questions
- 10 min: scenario questions
- 10 min: logistics, budget, timeline, and candidate questions

---

## Opening Script

"We're building HexaVerse as a long-form educational universe that starts with children's science storybooks and grows into systems thinking, Mars engineering, AI, and future civilization design. We're looking for an illustrator who can help build a lasting visual identity rather than only executing one book. We'd like to understand how you work, how you think about character continuity, and how you approach educational storytelling."

---

## Portfolio Walkthrough Questions

1. Which 2-3 pieces in your portfolio best reflect the kind of work you would bring to this project?
2. Show us an example where you kept a recurring character consistent across multiple illustrations.
3. Show us an example where the image had to teach or clarify something, not just look appealing.
4. How do you balance warmth and accessibility with visual sophistication?
5. Which part of your current portfolio is closest to the HexaVerse tone, and which part is furthest from it?

### What to listen for
- Can the candidate explain design choices clearly?
- Do they understand audience and reading level?
- Do they have actual examples of consistency, not just isolated strong images?

---

## Process Questions

1. Walk us through your workflow from brief to final delivery.
2. At what stage do you prefer the most feedback: roughs, line art, or color comps?
3. How do you manage revisions across a multi-illustration project?
4. How do you keep character proportions, face structure, and emotional range consistent from scene to scene?
5. How do you organize source files and delivery packages?
6. What does your print production workflow look like?
7. How do you handle situations where the brief is clear on story but not yet visually resolved?

### What to listen for
- Uses a repeatable process, not improvisation only
- Knows where feedback belongs in the pipeline
- Understands file hygiene and delivery discipline
- Can explain consistency methods such as model sheets, expression sheets, color scripts, or proportion guides

---

## Educational Storytelling Questions

1. How do you illustrate scientific or technical ideas for children without becoming either too childish or too abstract?
2. If a scene has to explain gravity, orbit, or sound waves, how do you make the concept visible?
3. How do you decide what should be literal versus symbolic in an educational illustration?
4. What makes an illustration useful for a classroom or read-aloud setting?
5. How do you approach accessibility in illustrations for young learners?

### What to listen for
- Understands clarity as part of craft
- Talks about focus, hierarchy, labels, gesture, and scene logic
- Shows awareness of accessibility, representation, and comprehension

---

## Scenario Questions

### Scenario 1: Character Continuity
"Hexa appears as age 8 in the first arc, then ages into a 12-13 year old and later a 16-17 year old while staying instantly recognizable. How would you build that progression?"

### Scenario 2: Scientific Clarity
"A scene needs to show why planets orbit the Sun instead of falling into it. How would you compose that in a way a child can understand quickly?"

### Scenario 3: Feedback Tension
"You deliver strong work, but the curriculum team says the scene is beautiful and still not teaching the concept clearly enough. How do you respond?"

### Scenario 4: Production Risk
"Halfway through a book, you realize the approved character outfit is too detailed and is slowing the schedule. What do you do?"

### What to listen for
- Pragmatic problem solving
- Low ego, high clarity
- Ability to trade off detail versus consistency and schedule
- Strong collaboration instincts

---

## Logistics Questions

1. What is your availability over the next 3-6 months?
2. How many fully rendered illustrations can you realistically complete per week in a sustained way?
3. What are your rates for cover work, interiors, and revision rounds beyond scope?
4. Have you worked on multi-month or multi-book engagements before?
5. Are you comfortable delivering layered editable files?
6. What tools do you use most often?
7. What time zone do you work in, and how do you prefer to communicate?

---

## Candidate Questions To Expect

Be prepared to answer:
- How locked is the current art direction?
- Will there be a paid style test?
- Who gives final creative approval?
- How many stakeholders are in feedback loops?
- What is the decision timeline?
- How much scientific reference support is provided?
- Is there long-term continuity work beyond Book 1?

---

## Interview Close

"We'll review this conversation alongside your portfolio and our scorecard. If there's a fit, the next step will likely be either a paid style test or a direct discussion about the Book 1 pilot scope."
