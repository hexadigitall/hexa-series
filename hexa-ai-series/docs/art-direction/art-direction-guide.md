# HexaVerse Art Direction Guide

**Hexadigitall Technologies — April 2026**

---

## Overview

The HexaVerse is a visual world where children experience science, space, and systems thinking through a consistent, recognizable aesthetic. Every illustration—whether in books, courses, labs, or interactive media—should reinforce the brand promise: **"From Wonder to Wisdom: Learning the Frontier."**

This guide defines the visual identity, character design principles, world-building rules, and technical standards for all illustrated content across the HexaVerse.

---

## Visual Identity

### Color Philosophy

The HexaVerse uses a **bold, vibrant, accessible palette** rooted in the four learning arcs. Colors should feel energetic and futuristic, not dark or somber.

#### Arc Colors (Primary)

| Arc | Color | Hex | Usage |
|---|---|---|---|
| 🌟 The Galactic Playground | Purple | #7b2fff | Covers, borders, highlights (age 5-9) |
| 🚀 Mission to Mars | Orange | #ff6b35 | Covers, borders, highlights (age 10-14) |
| 🧠 The Silicon Astronaut | Cyan | #00c9ff | Covers, borders, highlights (age 15+) |
| 🛠️ Automating the Galaxy | Green | #9be564 | Covers, borders, highlights (advanced) |

#### Support Palette (Secondary)

| Color | Hex | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| Dark Navy (Background) | #0d0d2b | Deep space, scene backgrounds |
| Bright Cyan (Accent) | #00f5d4 | Light, energy, highlights |
| Pale Yellow (Warmth) | #fffacd | Suns, stars, warmth |
| Deep Purple (Shadow) | #2d1b4e | Depth, shadow, night |

**Accessibility:** All text and important elements must meet WCAG AA contrast ratios. Avoid pure red/green combinations; use purple/orange or cyan/warm yellow instead.

### Visual Style

#### Overall Aesthetic
- **Medium:** Illustrated (not photorealistic, not cartoon-simple)
- **Age Range:** Timeless quality that appeals to 5-year-olds and 15-year-olds
- **Tone:** Optimistic, curious, scientific, approachable
- **Reference:** Think: *Oklo/Katie Mazeika, Shaun Tan, BBC documentary illustrations, space-age children's books*

#### Line Work
- Clear, confident outlines (not sketchy or uncertain)
- Varied line weight to create depth and emphasis
- Bold outlines around characters and focal points
- Lighter outlines for background elements

#### Composition
- Rule of thirds; balanced but dynamic
- Character often positioned to engage (looking at reader, active pose)
- Rich background detail that supports learning (labels, annotations, small story beats)
- Negative space used intentionally (not overcrowded)

#### Detail Level
- Medium-to-high detail in focal areas (character faces, key objects)
- Simplified backgrounds unless background is pedagogically important
- Visible texture (cloud texture, metal texture, fabric folds) without photorealism
- Cleanliness and clarity over realism

### Typography in Illustrations

When text appears within illustrations:
- Use **Orbitron** for headings and labels (futuristic, clear)
- Use **Exo 2** for body text (readable, modern)
- Always provide high contrast (dark text on light, or light on dark)
- Keep type sizes consistent with the illustration size (readable when printed)

## Character Design

### Hexa: The Protagonist

#### Core Identity
- **Age progression:** Hexa appears at different ages across the arcs:
  - Galactic Playground (Books 1-5): 8 years old
  - Mission to Mars (Books 6-10): 12-13 years old
  - Silicon Astronaut (Books 11-14): 16-17 years old
  - Automating the Galaxy (Advanced): 18-22 years old
- **Gender:** Non-binary, designed to be relatable to all children
- **Appearance:** Approachable, thoughtful, slightly "tech-forward" without being robotic
- **Personality in appearance:** Curious eyes, relaxed posture, often wearing practical/futuristic clothing

#### Visual Characteristics

**Galactic Playground Hexa (Age 8)**
- Round, expressive face with large, curious eyes
- Natural hair texture, medium length (can be worn down or pulled back)
- Freckles or subtle skin texture for character
- Wearing practical, colorful clothing (cargo pants, tech-inspired jacket, or comfortable layered outfit)
- Footwear: sneakers or practical boots (age-appropriate)
- Small technological accessory (wrist band, goggles, data glove) that hints at the future

**Mission to Mars Hexa (Age 12-13)**
- Same facial structure, now slightly more angular
- More confident posture; often in action poses
- Clothing: Mission-ready outfit (possibly suit-adjacent, but not militaristic)
- Accessories: More visible tech (enhanced wrist device, tool belt, communication device)
- Physical capability: Shown climbing, exploring, problem-solving

**Silicon Astronaut Hexa (Age 16-17)**
- Adult-like proportions; confident, purposeful
- More visible technological integration (augmentations are subtle but visible)
- Clothing: Professional-futuristic (lab coat, suit with tech elements, or mission-ready gear)
- Accessories: Advanced tech (neural interface indicator, advanced wrist device, tools)
- Physical capability: Leading teams, making decisions, teaching

#### Hexa Design Rules
1. **Consistency:** The same Hexa should be recognizable across all books, just aging appropriately
2. **Inclusivity:** No single "true" look; Hexa can be illustrated with different skin tones, hair textures, and body types (artist choice), but facial proportions and personality should remain consistent
3. **Clothing:** Changes by book but always feels like the same person; builds a wardrobe across the series
4. **Emotion:** Hexa's expressions should vary widely—curious, excited, frustrated, determined, thoughtful
5. **Posture:** Energetic and active (not sitting passively); often shown learning, experimenting, or teaching

### Secondary Characters

#### Mentor Figure: "The Guide"

Each arc should have a consistent mentor character who appears across books.

**Galactic Playground Mentor: "Professor Nova"**
- Age: Adult (40s-50s)
- Role: Wise, patient scientist who explains concepts
- Appearance:
  - Warm, approachable expression
  - Practical clothing (lab coat, or field-ready outfit)
  - Carries tools of learning (clipboard, telescope, measuring device)
  - Optional: Subtle technological augmentation (smart glasses, wrist device)
- Visual consistency: Same mentor should appear in Books 1-5, aging slightly if needed

**Mission to Mars Mentor: "Commander Chen"**
- Age: Adult (40s-60s)
- Role: Engineering-focused mentor; mission leader
- Appearance:
  - Confident, experienced expression
  - Mission suit or technical clothing
  - Carries mission-critical tools (device, map, instruments)
- Visual consistency: Command presence; consistent across Books 6-10

**Silicon Astronaut Mentor: "Dr. Keene"**
- Age: Adult (40s-50s, possibly with visible augmentations)
- Role: Advanced researcher; AI and bioengineering expert
- Appearance:
  - Intelligent, forward-thinking expression
  - Advanced technical clothing (research suit, neural interface visible)
  - Carries speculative tools (holographic device, genetic analyzer)
- Visual consistency: Reflects advanced technology; consistent across Books 11-14

#### Secondary Characters and Companions

Beyond the main mentor, each book should introduce consistent secondary characters who may appear across multiple titles:

- **"Zephyr"** — A robot/AI companion (appears in multiple books; design stays consistent)
- **"Maya"** — A peer character (appears across Galactic Playground; ages appropriately in later arcs)
- **"Koji"** — Another peer character (appears across multiple arcs; loyal, pragmatic)
- **"Echo"** — A data-focused AI character (appears in later arcs; visual representation should be distinctive)

**Secondary Character Design Rules:**
1. Give each character a consistent color association or clothing signature
2. Design them to be easily recognizable and reusable
3. Ensure character diversity (age, ability, appearance, background)
4. Show characters in different emotional states and contexts

## World-Building and Background Elements

### Settings

#### Galactic Playground Worlds
- **Earth scenes:** Parks, homes, classrooms, playgrounds (grounded and familiar)
- **Space scenes:** Starfield backgrounds, planets, simple spacecraft
- **Color scheme:** Lots of purple (#7b2fff) as accent; bright, accessible backgrounds
- **Detail level:** Simplified but rich; visible labels and annotations

#### Mission to Mars Worlds
- **Mars surface:** Rusty-red landscape with visible atmosphere, distant horizon
- **Spacecraft interiors:** Technical, functional, with visible life support systems
- **Habitats:** Dome structures, pressurized environments, visible technology
- **Color scheme:** Lots of orange (#ff6b35) as accent; warm, industrial feel
- **Detail level:** Medium-to-high technical detail (but not cluttered)

#### Silicon Astronaut Worlds
- **Off-world colonies:** Advanced structures, visible AI systems, bioengineered elements
- **Neural interfaces:** Visualizations of AI-human interaction (can be abstract or concrete)
- **Biotech environments:** Labs, genetic visualization, organisms
- **Color scheme:** Lots of cyan (#00c9ff) as accent; futuristic, sleek, technical
- **Detail level:** High detail with speculative elements; future-forward

#### Automating the Galaxy Worlds
- **Planetary infrastructure:** Large-scale systems, orbital mechanics, power distribution
- **Autonomous systems:** Visualizations of coordinated robots, AI networks
- **Frontier settlements:** Mature off-world habitats
- **Color scheme:** Lots of green (#9be564) as accent; systems-focused, interconnected
- **Detail level:** Complex but clear; infographics and systems diagrams integrated

### Repeated Elements (World Consistency)

To reinforce a cohesive universe:

1. **Hexa's home:** Should appear consistently (evolve from house to lab across arcs)
2. **Spacecraft design language:** Establish a design vocabulary for ships; reuse and iterate
3. **Technology aesthetic:** Fonts, button styles, holographic elements should feel consistent
4. **Flora and fauna:** If creatures or plants appear, maintain consistent design across books
5. **Celestial bodies:** Earth, Moon, Mars, and other planets should look consistent across all illustrations

## Illustration Technical Specifications

### File Requirements

#### Resolution and Format
- **Minimum resolution:** 300 DPI at final print size (8.5" x 11" = 2550 x 3300 pixels at 300 DPI)
- **File format:** High-res TIFF or PSD (layered for edits)
- **Color space:** RGB (for digital), CMYK (for print—convert after approval)
- **Bleed:** 0.25" bleed on all sides for print files

#### Deliverables
1. **High-res file:** 300 DPI TIFF (final version)
2. **Print-ready PDF:** CMYK, with bleed, for print production
3. **Web version:** 72 DPI JPG or PNG for digital display
4. **Source file:** Layered PSD or AI (for client edits/revisions)

### Production Standards

#### Cover Illustration
- Full-page illustration (front cover)
- Must include:
  - Hexa as focal point
  - Arc color as dominant background or accent
  - Title text area (safe zone of at least 1.5" from edges)
  - Sense of the book's core concept
- Multiple mood options may be requested (excitement, discovery, wonder)

#### Interior Illustrations
- Minimum 12 illustrations per book (at least one per chapter)
- Can be full-page, half-page, or spot illustrations
- Must include:
  - Clear scene and characters
  - Supports the text narrative
  - At least one "teaching moment" illustration (labeled diagram, annotated scene)
- Density: Avoid overcrowding; use white space intentionally

#### Illustration Placement
- Illustrations should be placed to break up text and support comprehension
- At least one illustration per 2-3 pages
- No illustrations should obscure critical text

### Revision and Approval Process

1. **Sketches (pencil or digital rough):** Artist submits pencil roughs for content approval
   - Client feedback: Pose, composition, character accuracy, scene elements
2. **Line art:** Ink/finalize line work
   - Client feedback: Detail, consistency, readability
3. **Color comps:** Flat colors, no shading
   - Client feedback: Color choices, arc-color usage, accessibility
4. **Final illustrations:** Fully rendered with shading, texture, effects
   - Client sign-off: Quality check, technical spec verification

**Revision limit:** 2 rounds of revisions per illustration (beyond that, charge per revision)

## Accessibility Standards

### Color Accessibility
- No red/green combinations without additional differentiation
- Minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio for text and important elements
- Test illustrations with color-blindness simulators (Coblis, Color Oracle)

### Illustration Clarity
- Characters and objects should be clearly identifiable even in small sizes (web thumbnails)
- Important details should not rely solely on color; use shape and texture too
- Alt text provided for all illustrations: descriptive, concise, 100-150 characters

### Representation
- Diverse character appearances, abilities, body types, and backgrounds across the series
- No stereotypical or offensive representations
- If illustrating disability or diversity, do so thoughtfully and authentically

## Brand Consistency Checklist

Every illustration should pass this checklist before final delivery:

- ✓ Uses correct arc color as primary or dominant accent
- ✓ Hexa (or secondary character) is recognizable and age-appropriate for arc
- ✓ Visual style matches reference (confident lines, medium detail, optimistic tone)
- ✓ Text (if any) uses Orbitron or Exo 2 fonts with high contrast
- ✓ 300 DPI at final size; RGB color space
- ✓ Contains descriptive alt text
- ✓ Passes WCAG AA contrast check
- ✓ Represents diversity and inclusivity where applicable
- ✓ Supports the text narrative and learning objective

## Illustrator Engagement

### Preferred Qualifications
- Children's book illustration experience (ages 5-17)
- Ability to work in consistent style across 12+ pieces per book
- Comfortable with technical/scientific subject matter
- Strong understanding of composition, color theory, and accessibility
- Experience with digital illustration and 300 DPI file production

### Preferred Tools
- Procreate, Clip Studio Paint, or Adobe Creative Suite
- Comfortable delivering layered source files (PSD or AI)
- Can work with reference materials and style guides

### Engagement Model (Recommended)
1. **Onboarding:** 2-week character design and style development
2. **Book batching:** Illustrator takes 3-5 books at a time
3. **Revision process:** 2 rounds of revisions per illustration
4. **Quality assurance:** Client does final DPI/color check before print
