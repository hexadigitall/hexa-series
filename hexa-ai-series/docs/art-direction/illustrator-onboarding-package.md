## The HexaVerse Story

### What We're Building

The **Hexa-AI-Series** is an expanding educational universe that teaches children and young adults to think like builders of future civilizations. It starts with wonder (why does an apple fall?) and grows into hard systems thinking (how do we take Earth with us to Mars?).

The series has:
- **4 learning arcs** (Galactic Playground, Mission to Mars, Silicon Astronaut, Automating the Galaxy)
- **14+ foundational story titles** that introduce core concepts
- **Planned expansion** into courses, labs, and capstones
- **1 protagonist:** Hexa, who grows with learners from age 8 to age 22+

### Your Role

You're bringing the HexaVerse to life through illustrations. Your work will:
1. Make abstract science concepts visible and understandable
2. Establish a consistent, recognizable visual identity across 14+ books
3. Represent diverse characters and authentic perspectives
4. Support learners of all abilities through clear, accessible visual communication

This is a long-term partnership. We're planning 12-24 months of collaboration, starting with Books 1-5.

## Key Creative Principles

### 1. Consistency Across Books

Hexa should be instantly recognizable in every book. Same face structure, same personality in appearance, same signature clothing item.

**Why it matters:** Learners bond with Hexa across the series. A child reading Book 1 at age 7 might read Book 5 at age 9 and Book 10 at age 14. Hexa should be the constant thread.

### 2. Age-Appropriate Design

Hexa ages across the arcs, but the aging should feel natural, not sudden.

**Example progression:**
- Book 1-5: Hexa age 8 (round face, energetic, small)
- Book 6-10: Hexa age 12-13 (angular features developing, athletic, confident)
- Book 11-14: Hexa age 16-17 (adult proportions, leadership presence)
- Book 15+: Hexa age 18+ (fully adult, possibly with visible tech augmentation)

### 3. Active Learning Postures

Hexa is not sitting passively. Hexa is climbing, pointing, building, experimenting, teaching. Visual communication should show *agency*—Hexa makes things happen.

### 4. Emotional Authenticity

Characters should express a full range of emotions: curiosity, excitement, frustration, determination, wonder, and sometimes doubt. Don't sanitize emotions; learners connect with honest feeling.

### 5. Scientific Accuracy

Illustrations must support, never contradict, the text. If the book says gravity pulls downward, the illustration should show that. If the book says Mars is rusty, Mars should look rusty.

**Note:** We'll provide science fact-checking; you focus on visual accuracy.

### 6. Inclusive Representation

The HexaVerse is for all learners. Characters should reflect real diversity:
- Varied skin tones, hair textures, and facial features
- Characters with different abilities (mobility, vision, hearing)
- Gender diversity (non-binary, trans, and cisgender characters)
- Different body types and presentations
- Various family structures and backgrounds

Representation should be authentic, not tokenized. When in doubt, ask us.

## Visual Brand Identity at a Glance

### Color Palette

**Arc Colors (primary):**
- Galactic Playground: #7b2fff (purple)
- Mission to Mars: #ff6b35 (orange)
- Silicon Astronaut: #00c9ff (cyan)
- Automating the Galaxy: #9be564 (green)

**Support Colors:**
- Dark Navy #0d0d2b (backgrounds, deep space)
- Bright Cyan #00f5d4 (accents, light, energy)
- Pale Yellow #fffacd (suns, warmth)
- Deep Purple #2d1b4e (shadows, depth)

**Accessibility:** Every illustration must pass WCAG AA contrast checks. Test colors with color-blindness simulators (Coblis, Color Oracle).

### Style

- **Medium:** Illustrated (not photorealistic, not cartoon-simple)
- **Lines:** Clear, confident outlines with varied line weight
- **Detail:** Medium-to-high in focal areas; simplified backgrounds
- **Tone:** Optimistic, curious, scientific, approachable
- **References:** Study Oklo, Katie Mazeika, Shaun Tan, BBC documentaries, space-age children's books

### Typography

When text appears in illustrations:
- **Headings/labels:** Orbitron (futuristic, clear)
- **Body text:** Exo 2 (readable, modern)
- **Accessibility:** High contrast (dark on light or light on dark)

## Production Phases Overview

### Phase 1: Design Kickoff (Week 1)
- You receive manuscript, briefs, and references
- You confirm understanding and propose style approach
- Team approves before you proceed to major production

### Phase 2: Pencil Roughs (Weeks 2-4)
- You deliver pencil sketches for all illustrations
- Team reviews for composition, accuracy, character consistency
- Feedback: approved or revision needed

### Phase 3: Line Art (Weeks 5-7)
- You finalize line work with clean, confident outlines
- Includes varied line weight for depth
- Team reviews for consistency and clarity

### Phase 4: Color Comps (Weeks 8-9)
- You deliver flat color (no shading) to verify color choices
- Arc color must be clearly present
- Team reviews for mood, accessibility, consistency

### Phase 5: Final Rendering (Weeks 10-12)
- You add shading, texture, effects, and polish
- Should look professional and publication-ready
- Team final review before technical QA

### Phase 6: Technical QA (Week 13)
- Files verified for DPI, color mode, bleed, naming
- Alt text and accessibility checked
- Files archived and delivered

**Timeline:** 12-16 weeks per book (~3-4 months)

**Revisions included:** 2 rounds per illustration (pencil and/or color phase)

**Additional revisions:** Billable at hourly rate

## Technical Specifications

### File Requirements

**For delivery:**
- High-res TIFF: 300 DPI, RGB, at print size (2550 x 3300 pixels for 8.5" x 11")
- Layered PSD/AI with organized layers (sketch, line, base color, shading, effects)
- Print-ready PDF in CMYK with 0.25" bleed
- Web version JPG at 72 DPI

**During production:**
- You can work at any size/resolution you prefer
- Export as TIFF at 300 DPI when delivering
- Provide layered source for edits

### File Naming Convention

```
[book-slug]_[chapter-number]_[scene-description]_[version].[ext]

Examples:
why-does-the-apple-fall_01_apple-falling_v1.psd
why-does-the-apple-fall_01_apple-falling_final.tif
why-does-the-apple-fall_cover_galactic-playground_v2.psd
```

### Tools

You should be comfortable with:
- Digital illustration software (Procreate, Clip Studio Paint, Adobe Creative Suite)
- Exporting at 300 DPI in multiple formats (TIFF, PSD, PDF)
- Creating layered files (we may need to edit or adjust later)
- Color management (RGB vs CMYK conversion)

## Feedback and Communication Protocol

### Giving Feedback (What We'll Do)

We will:
- [ ] Be specific: "The pose feels passive; can Hexa reach toward something?"
- [ ] Explain why: "This shows active learning because the text says Hexa is experimenting"
- [ ] Suggest alternatives: "Try this angle, or use this reference if you prefer"
- [ ] Acknowledge what's working: "Love the expression; the detail is perfect"
- [ ] Respond within 3-5 business days

### Receiving Feedback (What We Expect)

When you get feedback:
- Ask clarifying questions if something is ambiguous
- Propose solutions: "I can try this, or this reference might help"
- Communicate blockers early: "This needs more research" or "Timeline might shift 2-3 days"
- Deliver revisions within agreed timeframe
- Keep version control updated

### Communication Cadence

- Weekly check-in calls (30-60 min)
- Asynchronous feedback via shared document or email
- Project management tool for deliverable tracking

## Curriculum and Learning Context

### Why This Matters

Illustrations in educational books are not decoration. They're primary teaching tools.

- Visual learners may understand a concept better from the illustration than the text
- Illustrations set the emotional tone and engage curiosity
- Consistent visual identity helps learners feel safe exploring challenging ideas
- Diverse, authentic representation shows all learners they belong

Your illustrations will directly impact learning outcomes. Learners will study these images, discuss them in classrooms, and remember them years later.

### You're Not Just Making Pretty Pictures

You're building a visual language that helps children think about gravity, Mars, AI, and human futures. You're showing them that science is accessible, exciting, and full of possibility.
