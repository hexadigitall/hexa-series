# Why Does the Apple Fall?

## Chapter 1: Hexa Wonders

### Hook
Have you ever dropped something and watched it fall to the ground? Why does it fall *down* instead of up or to the side?

### Explain
Long ago, a scientist named Isaac Newton was sitting under a tree when an apple fell on his head. He started wondering: *Why did that apple fall?* He realized something invisible was pulling the apple toward the Earth. He called this invisible force **gravity**.

Gravity is like an invisible rope that pulls objects toward Earth. Every object on Earth—apples, you, your toys—feels this pull. It always pulls downward, toward the ground.

### Show
*[ILLUSTRATION: Young Hexa looking up at an apple tree, with an apple falling toward the ground. A glowing arrow shows the direction of gravity pulling downward.]*

When you throw a ball up in the air, it doesn't stay floating. Gravity pulls it back down. When you jump, you always come back down. Gravity is everywhere!

### Try
**Activity: Drop and Observe**
- Find three objects: a small toy, a feather, and a coin
- Drop them from the same height
- Watch what happens
- Do they all fall? Do they all fall at the same speed?

### Reflect
*What is one thing you dropped today that gravity pulled down?*

### Check
**Question:** Why did Newton's apple fall from the tree?
- A) Because wind pushed it
- B) Because invisible gravity pulled it toward Earth
- C) Because the apple was tired

**Answer:** B

---

## Chapter 2: The Force of Gravity

### Hook
Gravity is a **force**. A force is a push or a pull that makes things move or change. What forces can you think of?

### Explain
Gravity is a pulling force. It doesn't push; it pulls everything toward the center of the Earth. Even though you can't see gravity, you can see what it does.

The bigger an object is, the stronger its pull of gravity. Earth is very big, so its gravity is strong. That's why you don't float away!

### Show
*[ILLUSTRATION: Earth (blue and green) with arrows pointing inward from all directions, showing gravity pulling toward the center. A small figure standing on Earth's surface.]*

Imagine Earth as a giant magnet pulling everything toward it. That's similar to how gravity works!

### Try
**Activity: Jump and Feel Gravity**
- Jump as high as you can
- Notice how gravity pulls you back down
- Try again, but this time imagine you're on the Moon (which has weaker gravity)
- You would jump higher on the Moon because its gravity is weaker!

### Reflect
*If gravity is a pulling force, what does it pull toward?*

### Check
**Question:** Is gravity a push or a pull?
- A) A push
- B) A pull
- C) Both

**Answer:** B

## Chapter 3: Gravity Everywhere

### Hook
Gravity doesn't just work on Earth. It's everywhere in space!

### Explain
Gravity exists on other planets, moons, and even around stars. But every planet and moon has *different* gravity.

- **Earth:** Our home. Strong gravity. That's why we stay on the ground.
- **The Moon:** Much smaller. Weaker gravity. Astronauts can jump very high!
- **Jupiter:** Much bigger. Stronger gravity. You would feel much heavier there!

### Show
*[ILLUSTRATION: A small figure on Earth, the Moon (where they're floating higher), and a dark Jupiter, showing how the same figure would feel different weight on each.]*

### Try
**Activity: Imagine Different Gravity**
- On Earth, you weigh one unit of "weight"
- On the Moon, you would weigh 1/6 of that (because the Moon is smaller and its gravity is weaker)
- If you weigh 60 pounds on Earth, you would weigh only 10 pounds on the Moon!

### Reflect
*Would you like to live on a planet with stronger or weaker gravity? Why?*

### Check
**Question:** Why would you jump higher on the Moon than on Earth?
- A) Because the Moon has stronger gravity
- B) Because the Moon has weaker gravity
- C) Because there's no air on the Moon

**Answer:** B

## Chapter 4: Gravity and Space

### Hook
What happens to gravity if you go far away from Earth? Does it go away?

### Explain
Gravity gets weaker the farther away you are from Earth, but it never completely disappears. Even astronauts in space feel Earth's gravity pulling on them. That's why spacecraft have to be launched with enough speed to break away from Earth's pull.

Gravity is also why planets orbit the Sun and moons orbit planets. Gravity holds everything in place!

### Show
*[ILLUSTRATION: The Sun with planets orbiting around it in circular paths. Arrows showing gravity pulling each planet toward the Sun, but the planet's speed keeps it circling rather than falling in.]*

### Try
**Activity: Spinning Bucket (with parent supervision)**
- Ask a parent to swing a bucket of water in a circle
- The water doesn't fall out because the motion and gravity work together
- This is similar to how planets orbit the Sun!

### Reflect
*If gravity disappeared, what would happen to the Moon? To you?*

### Check
**Question:** Why do planets orbit the Sun instead of falling into it?
- A) Because gravity pulls them toward the Sun, but their speed keeps them circling
- B) Because gravity doesn't exist in space
- C) Because planets are held by invisible strings

**Answer:** A

## Glossary

**Gravity:** An invisible force that pulls objects toward Earth and other large objects in space.

**Force:** A push or a pull that makes something move or change direction.

**Orbit:** To move around something else in a circular path. The Moon orbits Earth; planets orbit the Sun.

**Weight:** How heavy something is. Weight depends on gravity. You would weigh less on the Moon because gravity is weaker there.

**Acceleration:** When something speeds up, slows down, or changes direction. Gravity causes acceleration by pulling objects downward.

**Newton:** A scientist who discovered that gravity is a force. He lived over 300 years ago and came up with ideas about how the universe works.

**Planet:** A large object that orbits a star. Earth is a planet that orbits the Sun.

**Moon:** A smaller object that orbits a planet. Earth has one Moon.

## Real-World Connection

**Did you know?**
Astronauts train for missions to space by going into airplanes that simulate zero gravity. For a few seconds at a time, they experience what it's like when gravity doesn't pull them down. It feels like floating!

When astronauts go to the International Space Station, they're still experiencing Earth's gravity—but they're moving so fast in a circle around Earth that they feel weightless. It's the same idea as the spinning bucket!

## Teacher and Parent Guide

### Learning Outcomes
By the end of this book, students will be able to:
1. Define gravity as a force that pulls objects toward Earth
2. Explain why objects fall
3. Understand that gravity differs on different planets
4. Apply gravity concepts to real-world scenarios

### Discussion Questions
- "What would happen if there was no gravity?"
- "Why don't we fall off the Earth if it's round?"
- "How does gravity help keep the Moon orbiting Earth?"

### Extension Activities
- **Gravity Science Fair:** Explore how different objects fall. Do heavier objects fall faster?
- **Moon Simulation:** Calculate how high you could jump on the Moon
- **Build a Gravity Maze:** Create a track where gravity makes a ball roll through obstacles

- Large, readable font suitable for early readers
- High-contrast illustrations for visual clarity
- Simple sentence structures with consistent vocabulary
- Illustrations support text for all learners
